$hds THE JEWISH CALENDAR $hde
$l $sl1                                                                                                 
     This year is 5765 because, according to Biblical chronology, the
world was created 5765 years ago.
     The Jewish calendar is calculated according to both lunar and
solar cycles.  The months and festival dates are determined by the
phases of the moon, while the seasons are based on the revolution of
the earth around the sun.  This dual system is necessary because the
twelve-month lunar year is approximately eleven days shorter than the
solar year, or 354 days to the solar year's 365 days.  Using a wholly
lunar calendar for each solar year, the Jewish holidays would fall
approximately eleven days earlier each year.  Eventually, Pesac//h,
which occurs at the spring harvest, would fall in the middle of the
winter, while Sukkot, which occurs at the autumn harvest, would come
in July. Therefore, to compensate for the differences between the
lunar and solar years, and to keep the holidays in their proper
season, an extra month is periodically added to the lunar calendar. 
This "leap month," Ada//r II, is added seven times in a
nineteen-year-cycle--in the third, sixth, eighth, eleventh,
fourteenth, seventeenth, and nineteenth years--and keeps the
discrepancy between the lunar and solar calendars at a minimum.
     Originally, months were simply designated by numbers.  After the
Babylonian Exile (586-536 B.C.E.), specific names for the months
became more common and accepted.  The names of the months and their
approximate English counterparts are: Tis//hrei--October;
C//hes//hvan--November; Kislev--December; Tevet--January;
S//hevat--February; Ada//r--March; Nisan--April; Iya//r--May;
Sivan--June; Tammuz--July; Av--August; and Elul--September.  Some
Hebrew months have twenty-nine days; some have thirty days; and,
depending on the year, some have either twenty-nine or thirty days. 
Nisan, Sivan, Av, Tis//hrei, S//hevat and--in a leap year--Ada//r I, are
thirty-day months.  Iya//r, Tammuz, Elul, Tevet and Ada//r (Ada//r II in
leap years) are twenty-nine day months.  C//hes//hvan and Kislev vary.
     Ros//h C//hodes//h celebrates the new moon and, therefore, the
new month.  When a month has thirty days, there are two days of
Ros//h C//hodes//h: one on the thirtieth day of the previous month
and one on the first day of the new month.  A month having twenty-nine
days has only a one-day Ros//h C//hodes//h, on the first day of the
new month.  Ros//h C//hodes//h is celebrated as a minor festival with
special insertions in the prayer services.

     Jewish and secular calendar dates for all festivals and fasts,
including Ros//h C//hodes//h, are listed after each month's
calendar.  In the Jewish calendar, the day begins and ends at sundown.
This means that if the secular date listed for the onset of a
festival is e.g., March 15, the festival begins the previous evening,
after sunset, on March 14.
     Holiday Torah readings which are not listed in the monthly
calendars are listed--with their Hafta//rot--before the month of
Tis//hrei.
     Candle lighting times to mark the onset of S//hab//bat on Friday
nights are based on Eastern Standard and Daylight Savings Time in New
York City.  
     The Hebrew names of the Five Books of the Torah are used for the
S//hab//bat readings:  "Genesis" is $g1 BERESHIT; $g2 "Exodus" is S//HEMOT;
"Leviticus" is VAYIKRA; "Numbers" is $g1 BEMIDBAR; $g2 and "Deuteronomy" is
DEVA//RIM.
     In this calendar, the upper number represents the English date
and the lower number, its Hebrew equivalent.  A line of dashes
separates each week of the month.  Hebrew dates of special importance
are preceded by the letter &+x.
